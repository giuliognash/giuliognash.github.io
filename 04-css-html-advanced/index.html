Detection of Rodenticides in Coyotes

By: Giulio Gnash

December 5, 2025

Paul Stapp's research into rodenticides increasingly points to how normalized poison has become in urban life—and how deeply it travels through ecosystems. His recent study notes, “The near‐universal exposure of coyotes in southern California to ARs reflects how widespread and acceptable it is to apply rodenticides to control rodents perceived as pests.” Easy retail access, online sales, and the demand for fast, cheap solutions have made chemical control the default, even as the ecology of urban rodents remains poorly understood. Despite environmental awareness campaigns, many residents continue to prioritize immediate relief over long-term ecological risk, often maintaining permanent bait stations that lead to overuse. Coyotes, with their omnivorous diets and tolerance for human development, have become unintended witnesses to this contamination. As the study explains, “Because of their omnivorous habits and tolerance for human development, coyotes can be useful sentinels of environmental contamination from ARs and other pollutants.”
